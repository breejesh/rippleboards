# RippleBoards

## Abstract
This research examines the disproportionately high age-adjusted premature mortality rate among low-income Black adults living below 150% of the federal poverty level. According to CDC mortality data (2024), the age-adjusted death rate for non-Hispanic Black individuals was approximately 910 compared to 759 per 100,000 for non-Hispanic White individuals. This persistent gap reflects structural inequities rather than individual behavioral differences alone. This disparity is associated with the intersection of housing instability and digital exclusion. Based on the report by Harvard University, 32% of Black renter households are severely cost-burdened (spending more than 30% of income on housing), compared to approximately 22% of White renter households. In addition, national data indicates approximately 38% of households below the poverty line lack fixed broadband access, compared to 15-24% among higher-income households. Research further identifies broadband access as a “super-determinant” where limited digital infrastructure correlates with lower telehealth engagement and higher preventable mortality in minority census tracts. Together, data demonstrate that low-income Black communities experience disproportionate exposure to structural barriers correlated with elevated premature mortality.
Research Question: How can an interactive Decision Support System (DSS) like RippleBoards enable public health officials to simulate the projected impact of corrective interventions on premature mortality in low-income Black communities?
We will utilize CDC PLACES and ACS 5-year estimates [3] to map overlaps between housing costs and broadband access. Literature will be sourced from PubMed and Google Scholar using terms like "digital redlining" and "housing stability interventions," validated by Healthy People 2030 causal frameworks.
For our key informant interview, we will seek a U.S.-based public health researcher or data analyst who has published on these issues. Potential contacts will be identified through recent peer-reviewed publications, university faculty directories, and institutional research pages. We will establish contact through email outreach and LinkedIn to secure a virtual interview.
RippleBoards functions as a DSS "What-If" engine. Unlike static maps, it allows users to "drop" corrective interventions, such as broadband grants or housing subsidies, into a virtual census tract. Using PLACES data, the system simulates "ripples" projecting how these actions increase healthcare utilization and reduce predicted mortality, allowing for risk-free policy testing.
